Summer Flight Delays in the U.S.
What the Data Tells Travelers

DS4200: Information Presentation and Data Visualization  ·  Final Project  ·  Vicente Santos, Emilia Santos, and Brett Mezrow

Introduction

We analyzed a dataset of U.S. domestic flights in 2015 in an attempt to understand delay patterns during peak summer travel — the three busiest months for american air travel. Our analysis will focus on three core questions: Which airlines have the worst delay records, and what is driving said delays? When during the day and week are delays most likely to happen? And where, geographically, are delays most concentrated?

Delays cost travelers time, money and productivity. By understanding the underlying structural patterns — operational cascades, time-of-day compounding, and geographic hotspots — travelers can make smarter booking decisions. This project demonstrates that a handful of structural factors explain the majority of delays, and that awareness of these patterns provides real, actionable value for anyone planning summer travel.

Data Description

Our dataset comes from the Bureau of Transportation Statistics via Kaggle, covering all U.S. domestic flights in 2015. The original dataset is at least 5.8 million flights across 31 features, 14 airlines, and 322 airports. For this analysis, we filtered to the summer months (June–August), restricted to the 20 busiest origin airports by departure count, and sampled approximately 10,000 rows for visualization performance.

Key features include departure and arrival delays, five delay-cause breakdowns (carrier, weather, NAS, late aircraft, security), flight distance, and scheduled departure time. One limitation of this dataset is the absence of external weather severity and airport construction records — factors that would help disentangle operational delays from environmental ones in geographic comparisons, nevertheless, this dataset allows for meaningful comparison and analysis of flight data. An important note is that this dataset only includes flights from 2015, so the patterns observed may not reflect current conditions, especially post-pandemic changes in travel behavior and airline operations, but rather will paint us a picture of the structural dynamics of flight delays during a typical pre-pandemic summer season, which is more reflective of long-term trends.

01 / Airline On-Time Performance

Late aircraft delay is the dominant cause across nearly all airlines — delays are primarily operational, not weather-driven. Spirit Airlines leads at roughly 26% delayed, nearly 10 percentage points above the fleet-wide average. Top performers like Hawaiian Airlines minimize cascading delays through simpler point-to-point route structures with fewer opportunities for schedule propagation.

02 / Delay Heatmap: Hour × Day of Week

Delays compound through the day, peaking in late afternoon and evening (4–9 PM). Flights departing before 8 AM consistently show the lowest delays regardless of day of week. Weekday evenings are notably worse than weekends — likely because higher passenger and freight volume on business travel days accumulates earlier delays that ripple through afternoon and evening banks.

03 / Distance vs. Delay

Flight distance alone is a weak predictor of delay. Both short-haul and long-haul flights cluster near zero delay, but short-haul flights produce more extreme outliers — likely due to regional carriers operating at smaller airports with less operational resilience. Drag to brush any region of the scatter plot to explore the delay distribution for that specific subset of flights.

04 / U.S. Airport Delay Map

The main takeaway here is that not all major hubs perform equally. High-volume airports like ATL maintain lower average delays than similarly busy hubs like ORD and SFO, suggesting that general airport infrastructure, runawy configs, and local weather patterns matter more than raw traffic volume. Scroll to zoom, drag to pan and you can hover over any bubble for detailed stats.

05 / Daily Delay Trend by Airline

Delay spikes cluster around holiday weekends — most visibly July 4th — and weather events, but recovery speed varies significantly by airline. Some carriers return to baseline within a day while others remain elevated for a week, reflecting differences in fleet size, buffer scheduling, and operational recovery protocols. # Findings and conclusions

Findings & Conclusions

01 — The Domino Effect Is Real

Late aircraft delay — not weather — is the primary driver of flight delays across virtually all U.S. carriers. Airlines with tighter scheduling and fewer buffer minutes suffer disproportionately: one delayed inbound flight cascades into multiple delayed outbound flights throughout the day, compounding by the evening bank.

02 — Time of Day Matters More Than Day of Week

Morning departures before 8 AM are consistently the safest bet for on-time travel. Delays accumulate through the day as cascading effects build — a flight delayed at 7 AM affects the 10 AM rotation, which affects the 2 PM, and so on. This compounding pattern is consistent across all seven days of the week.

03 — Geography Isn’t Destiny

Some high-traffic hubs manage delays far better than others operating under comparable conditions, pointing to operational efficiency as the key differentiator. Hub design, staffing depth, and fleet rotation strategies appear to explain more variance than raw traffic volume or even local weather exposure.

Future work should incorporate weather severity indices and year-round flight data to disentangle operational delays from weather-driven events, and to determine whether the patterns observed in summer 2015 are representative of longer-term structural trends.
