Summer Flight Delays in the U.S.
What the Data Tells Travelers

DS4200: Information Presentation and Data Visualization  ·  Final Project  ·  Vicente Santos, Emilia Santos, and Brett Mezrow

Introduction

We analyzed a dataset of U.S. domestic flights in 2015 in an attempt to understand delay patterns during peak summer travel, the three busiest months for american air travel. Our analysis will focus on three core questions: Which airlines have the worst delay records, and what is driving said delays? When during the day and week are delays most likely to happen? And where, geographically, are delays most concentrated?

Delays cost travelers time, money and productivity. By understanding the underlying structural patterns, operational cascades, time-of-day compounding, and geographic hotspots, travelers can make smarter booking decisions. This project demonstrates that a handful of structural factors explain the majority of delays, and that awareness of these patterns provides real, actionable value for anyone planning summer travel.

Data Description

Our dataset comes from the Bureau of Transportation Statistics via Kaggle, covering all U.S. domestic flights in 2015. The original dataset is at least 5.8 million flights across 31 features, 14 airlines, and 322 airports. For this analysis, we filtered to the summer months (June - August), restricted to the 20 busiest origin airports by departure count, and sampled approximately 10,000 rows for visualization performance.

Key features of this dataset are departure and arrival delays, five delay-cause reasons, (carrier, weather, NAS, late aircraft, security), flight distance, and scheduled departure time. A notable limitation of this dataset is the lack of external weather data and airport maitenence data. These are both factors that would help distinguish operational delays from things like weather which are out of the airline's control. Nevertheless, this dataset allows for meaningful comparison and analysis of flight data. An important note is that this dataset only includes flights from 2015, so the patterns observed may not reflect current conditions, especially post-pandemic changes in travel behavior and airline operations, but rather will paint us a picture of the structural dynamics of flight delays during a typical pre-pandemic summer season, which is more reflective of long-term trends.

01 / Airline On-Time Performance
Late Aircraft — previous flight arrived late, leaving no time to turn the plane around for its next departure.
Carrier — airline-controlled issues such as crew scheduling, maintenance, or aircraft cleaning.
NAS — National Airspace System factors like heavy traffic volume, air traffic control, or non-severe weather.
Weather — significant meteorological conditions that directly prevented safe departure or arrival.
Security — checkpoint evacuation, re-screening, or a breach that caused a gate hold.

Spirit Airlines is clearly the worst performer, with over 38% of its flights delayed, more than double the rate of top performers Delta and Alaska, both at around 15%. The fleet-wide average is 23%. Late aircraft delay is the primary cause behind nearly every airline's delays, which shows that delays are mostly operational in nature. One delayed inbound flight leads to several delayed departures throughout the day. Hawaiian Airlines is an interesting exception. Its delays come entirely from carrier-controlled causes, with no late aircraft contribution, since it operates short, contained routes within Hawaii rather than a connected hub network where delays compound.

02 / Delay Heatmap: Hour × Day of Week

Delays accumulate throughout the day, peaking between 6 and 8 PM when average delays exceed 30 minutes on the worst weekday nights. Flights departing before 7 AM consistently have the fewest delays, averaging under 6 minutes regardless of the day of the week. Weekday evenings are notably worse than weekend evenings, likely due to the higher volume of passengers and cargo during the workweek causing a cascade effect through the afternoon and evening. Saturday and Sunday are the best days to fly, with evening delays significantly lower than what you would typically see Monday through Thursday.

03 / Distance vs. Delay

Flight distance does not have much correlation with delays, this is because short and long haul flights tend to cluster around zero minutes delayed, and a negative median delay indicates that most flights arrive on time or ahead of schedule. Where outliers do occur, they are distributed fairly evenly across all distance ranges. Outliers in the short haul segment are probably due to regional carriers whose operations are more constrained by smaller airports. Drag to brush any region of the scatter plot to explore the delay distribution for that specific subset of flights.

04 / U.S. Airport Delay Map

Hub airports can vary greatly. Atlanta is the busiest airport in the entire dataset, yet it isnt the most delayed, averaging just 12.4 minutes. This is notably lower than other busy hubs like Chicago at 16.1 minutes and SFO at 11.4 minutes. The two best performing airports are Salt Lake City at just 4 minutes and Minneapolis at 6 minutes on average. Infrastructure, runway layout, and local weather conditions appear to be bigger factors in determining delays than passenger traffic volume. Scroll to zoom, drag to pan, and hover over any bubble for detailed stats.

05 / Daily Delay Trend by Airline

Spirit is the clear outlier, regularly spiking past 150 minutes while the other four airlines stay within 0–60 minutes and tend to spike together. On the contrary to our hypothesis, July 4th was not the worst time in the summer. The biggest sustained delays align with early June and late August, when families flood airports at the start and end of summer break. Recovery speed varies too, with Southwest and Frontier bouncing back faster than JetBlue and United after disruptions. Findings & Conclusions

01: The Domino Effect Is Real

The primary driver of flight delays across the board is late aircraft delay, not weather. Airlines that operate with tight scheduling and few buffer minutes between flights were shown to have suffered delays disproportionately. One delayed flight in the morning cascades into multiple throughout the day.

02: Time of Day Matters More Than Day of Week

Morning departures that leave before 8AM are consistently on time. Delays tend to accumulate throughout the day as these cascading effects build. A flight delayed at 7AM will affect the 10AM rotation, which then affects the 2PM, and so on. This pattern of compounding is consistent across all seven days of the week.

03: Airports are not Everything.

Some high-traffic hubs manage delays far better than others operating under comparable conditions, pointing to operational efficiency as the key differentiator. Hub design, staffing depth, and fleet rotation strategies appear to explain more variance than raw traffic volume or even local weather exposure.

Future work should incorporate weather severity indices and year-round flight data to disentangle operational delays from weather-driven events, and to determine whether the patterns observed in summer 2015 are representative of longer-term structural trends.
